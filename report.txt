── 0.1 Thematic Domain ───────────────────────────────────────────────

  Theme: Finance, Monetary Policy & Capital Markets

  This project sits at the intersection of macroeconomics and machine
  learning, studying India's short-term money market — the mechanism
  through which the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) transmits its monetary
  policy stance to the broader economy.

── 0.2 Problem Definition ────────────────────────────────────────────

  The Weighted Average Call Money Rate (WACMR) is the overnight
  interbank lending rate at which scheduled commercial banks borrow
  and lend surplus funds among themselves. It is the operational
  target of RBI's Liquidity Adjustment Facility (LAF) and the
  first-order transmission channel of every Repo Rate change into
  the real economy — influencing borrowing costs for businesses,
  EMIs for households, bond yields, and currency movements.

  Despite its centrality, WACMR is poorly predicted by simple rule-
  based models because it is simultaneously driven by:
    (a) RBI's policy corridor (Repo, Reverse Repo, MSF rates),
    (b) system-level liquidity conditions (RBI balance sheet, M3),
    (c) short-term instrument flows (T-Bills, G-Secs, Commercial Paper),
    (d) inflation expectations (CPI indices), and
    (e) equity and forex market sentiment (Nifty 50, USD/INR).

  The interaction of these factors changes structurally across monetary
  regimes (e.g., pre-COVID tightening vs COVID-era accommodation),
  making the forecasting problem non-stationary and regime-dependent.

── 0.3 Importance ────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Accurate WACMR forecasting is important for three stakeholder groups:

  1. RBI / Monetary Policy: The WACMR-Repo spread is a real-time
     diagnostic for whether RBI's intended rate corridor is being
     enforced. Sustained deviations signal either surplus or deficit
     liquidity conditions requiring intervention.

  2. Institutional Investors (Banks, Mutual Funds, Treasuries):
     Short-term rate direction drives overnight fund (liquid fund)
     NAV movements, call money desk positioning, and duration risk
     on short-end bond portfolios. A 70%+ directional accuracy model
     is directly actionable for money market desks.

  3. Macroeconomic Research: Identifying the structural regime
     boundaries in India's liquidity management framework (pre- vs
     post-COVID) provides quantitative evidence for how exogenous
     shocks permanently shift the monetary transmission mechanism.

── 0.4 Research Objectives ───────────────────────────────────────────

  Objective 1 (Unsupervised): Identify statistically distinct monetary
    regimes in India's weekly money market data using PCA + K-Means
    clustering on RBI balance sheet and rate-corridor variables.

  Objective 2 (Supervised): Build a walk-forward XGBoost model to
    forecast 1-week-ahead WACMR and evaluate whether injecting regime
    labels as features improves prediction accuracy.

  Objective 3 (Interpretability): Use SHAP analysis to identify which
    categories of features (rate corridor, balance sheet, market flows,
    equity/forex sentiment) drive WACMR predictions and in what order.

  Objective 4 (Cross-Domain Enrichment): Supplement NDAP monetary data
    with external equity market (Nifty 50) and forex (USD/INR) data to
    capture market-sentiment signals absent from RBI datasets alone.

── 0.6 Cross-Domain Dataset Combination ──────────────────────────────

  The master panel deliberately spans four distinct data domains:

    Domain 1 — Monetary Policy Instruments (RBI rates, CRR, SLR)
    Domain 2 — Central Bank Balance Sheet (liabilities, forex reserves)
    Domain 3 — Short-Term Debt Market Flows (T-Bills, G-Secs, CP, Repo)
    Domain 4 — Macro Price Environment (CPI: headline, food, core, fuel)
    Domain 5 — Capital & Forex Markets (Nifty 50, USD/INR — external)

  This cross-domain design reflects the real-world mechanism: WACMR
  is not purely a function of the policy rate — it is the equilibrium
  outcome of all five forces operating simultaneously. A model trained
  only on rates would miss the liquidity-flow and sentiment channels.

── 0.7 Initial Data Exploration Findings ─────────────────────────────

  1. WACMR distribution is bimodal (peaks around ~6.5% and ~3.3%),
     immediately suggesting regime non-stationarity — confirmed by
     K-Means (K=2, Silhouette=0.4238).

  2. The 75% density filter removed 18 of 109 raw columns, primarily
     from the RBI balance sheet table where sub-categories had
     significant gaps prior to 2016.

  3. CPI indices are monthly; forward-fill (max 4 weeks) was applied.
     WPI and one sub-index were dropped (<63% density).

  4. T-Bill columns contained outstanding amounts, not yields (despite
     NDAP schema labelling). This was discovered during EDA and
     prompted sourcing of secondary-market T-Bill yields from the
     Ratios & Rates dataset instead.

  5. Nifty 50 and USD/INR weekly data has 100% density across the
     window with no imputation required.

  1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
========================================================================

This project demonstrates a two-phase machine learning pipeline to forecast
India's Weighted Average Call Money Rate (WACMR) — the primary overnight
interbank benchmark that reflects RBI's monetary policy transmission in real
time. Ten datasets were assembled: eight from the RBI/NDAP open-data API and
two external equity/forex sources (Nifty 50 and USD/INR OHLCV via Yahoo Finance,
enriched with five custom technical indicators). All data was aligned onto a
canonical Friday index spanning February 2014 to July 2024 (545 weeks), producing
a master panel of 117 features. A strict 75% density rule was applied to every
NDAP column; the external OHLCV columns achieved 100% density after indicator
warmup trimming.

In Phase 1 (Unsupervised), Principal Component Analysis (PCA) compressed the
feature space into components retaining 90%+ of the total variance. K-Means
clustering identified two statistically distinct monetary regimes: a "Normal /
Tightening" regime (Repo Rate ≈ 6.59%, Feb 2014–Feb 2020) and an
"Accommodation / Low-Rate" regime (Repo Rate ≈ 5.12%, Jan 2020–Jul 2024).
The boundary aligns precisely with the COVID-19 shock and the subsequent
structural shift in RBI's liquidity management framework.

In Phase 2 (Supervised), an XGBoost Regressor was evaluated using a
walk-forward expanding-window cross-validation protocol (minimum 156-week
training window, 1-week-ahead prediction, 389 test steps). The baseline model
achieved RMSE = 0.1019 and Directional Accuracy = 70.9%. Injecting K-Means
regime labels and cluster-centroid distances produced slightly worse results
(RMSE = 0.1044), confirming that XGBoost's internal tree splits implicitly
recover the same regime boundary from the autoregressive feature structure.
SHAP analysis revealed a critical additional finding: despite adding 28 equity
and forex market features (Nifty 50, USD/INR, and their technical indicators),
none appear in the top 15 SHAP drivers. The top five are entirely rate-corridor
variables — WACMR's own lag (t-1), the Repo Rate lag, the current Repo Rate,
the WACMR-Repo spread, and the MSF Rate — validating that India's call money
market is tightly LAF-corridor-bound and is not meaningfully influenced by
equity or currency market momentum at the weekly frequency.

  2. RESEARCH QUESTION & HYPOTHESIS
========================================================================

Research Question:
  Can distinct monetary liquidity regimes in India's money market — identified
  through unsupervised clustering of weekly RBI balance sheet composition,
  interbank rates, and short-term instrument flows — improve the prediction of
  the Weighted Average Call Money Rate (WACMR) when injected as a regime feature
  into a supervised gradient-boosted ensemble model?

Hypothesis Tested:
  H1 (supported):  India's weekly money market exhibits at least 2 statistically
      distinct macroeconomic regimes, identifiable from RBI balance sheet and
      rate-corridor data. CONFIRMED — K=2, Silhouette=0.4238.

  H2 (rejected):   Injecting regime labels into XGBoost will reduce RMSE by ≥15%.
      REJECTED — Regime-aware RMSE (0.1044) is 2.5% WORSE than baseline
      (0.1019). The model already learns regime structure implicitly from
      lagged rate-corridor features; adding explicit labels adds noise.

  H3 (supported):  The Repo Rate corridor variables (Repo, Reverse Repo, MSF)
      are the dominant predictors within the supervised phase.
      CONFIRMED — Ranks 2, 3, 5 in SHAP importance.

  H4 (new finding): Equity and forex market features (Nifty 50 OHLCV +
      technical indicators, USD/INR OHLCV + technical indicators) do not
      improve WACMR prediction at the weekly frequency.
      RESULT — None of the 28 new market-sentiment features appear in the
      top 15 SHAP drivers. The LAF rate corridor completely dominates.

── 4.1 Phase 1 — Unsupervised ML: Regime Discovery ───────────────────

  Algorithm        : StandardScaler → PCA → K-Means
  Feature matrix   : 86 numeric columns (target + target lags excluded)
  Residual NaN     : Imputed with column median (max 17.4% for 2 columns)
  PCA components   : 14  (90.4% variance retained)
  K sweep          : K = 2 to 7, evaluated by Silhouette Score

  Silhouette Scores:
    K=2  →  0.4238  ★ OPTIMAL
    K=3  →  0.4048
    K=4  →  0.3321
    K=5  →  0.3351
    K=6  →  0.3205
    K=7  →  0.3022

  Final K-Means    : K=2, n_init=15, max_iter=500, random_state=42
  Regime output    : regime_label (0 or 1) + cluster_dist_0, cluster_dist_1

     target_lag1 SHAP = 0.689 (Tightening) vs 0.344 (Accommodation).
     WACMR is significantly more predictable from its own recent history
     during tightening periods — in surplus regimes, discretionary RBI
     VRRR/VRR operations introduce more week-to-week noise.

  4. Current Repo Rate outranks Repo Rate lag in Regime 1 (#2 vs #3).
     In accommodation, the announced Repo Rate is the primary anchor
     for market expectations; in tightening, the recent trajectory
     of rate changes (lag structure) carries more weight.


── 5.4 Residual Analysis & Calendar Effects ─────────────────────────

  The "calendar effects" hypothesis — that FY-end (March) and Advance
  Tax outflow weeks (Sept 15, Dec 15, March 15, June 15) cause the
  model's largest errors — was tested and REJECTED.

  Calendar-event weeks  : 66 / 389 test steps
  RMSE on event weeks   : 0.0870   ← BETTER than average
  RMSE on non-event wks : 0.1046   ← WORSE than average

  The model actually performs BETTER during calendar-pressure weeks,
  not worse. This is interpretable: the seasonal liquidity tightenings
  at FY-end and advance-tax dates are well-established, recurring
  patterns that the model has learned from 10 years of training data.

  The 10 largest absolute errors are instead dominated by macro shocks:

  Date        Actual  Predicted   Error   Event
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  2020-04-03   4.329      4.990  −0.661   COVID emergency rate cut
  2021-05-28   3.178      3.790  −0.612   COVID second-wave peak
  2022-05-13   4.055      4.478  −0.422   Surprise 40bps RBI hike
  2022-06-10   4.341      3.999  +0.342   Adv Tax (Jun) ← only event
  2020-05-29   3.622      3.943  −0.321   COVID accommodation period
  2017-04-07   5.839      6.152  −0.314   Post-FY-end rebound
  2022-10-14   6.098      5.798  +0.300   Post-hike tightening
  2019-08-16   5.320      5.615  −0.295   Pre-COVID easing
  2022-09-23   5.451      5.160  +0.291   RBI rate hike cycle
  2020-03-27   5.051      5.309  −0.257   COVID + FY-End

  Revised Limitation: The model does not struggle with predictable
  seasonal patterns. It struggles with unpredictable policy
  discontinuities — emergency rate cuts, surprise inter-meeting hikes,
  and COVID-era structural breaks. These are, by definition, not
  forecastable from historical features alone. Adding event-dummy
  variables for extraordinary policy actions is the appropriate
  next step for reducing the tail errors.

  7. POLICY RECOMMENDATIONS & ACTIONABLE INSIGHTS
========================================================================

Based strictly on the data evidence, three concrete recommendations follow:

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RECOMMENDATION 1 — For RBI Liquidity Managers:
  Monitor the WACMR-Repo Spread as a real-time liquidity barometer.

  Evidence: SHAP rank #4 is the engineered feature (WACMR − Repo Rate),
  Mean |SHAP| = 0.072. During Regime 1 (COVID accommodation), this spread
  averaged −0.19% (WACMR consistently BELOW Repo), signalling persistent
  surplus liquidity. When the spread flips positive (WACMR > Repo), it is
  an early warning of system-level liquidity deficit that typically precedes
  rate hikes.

  Action: Operationalise a real-time WACMR-Repo spread dashboard. A sustained
  positive spread above +0.10% for 3 consecutive weeks should trigger an
  automatic liquidity review. The model assigns this spread a predictive
  weight of 0.072 SHAP — larger than the MSF Rate (0.058) itself.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RECOMMENDATION 2 — For Institutional Bond / Money Market Desks:
  Use the 70.9% Directional Accuracy with a regime-conditional position sizing
  strategy.

  Evidence: The walk-forward XGBoost correctly predicts rate direction (up/down)
  ~71% of the time across 389 out-of-sample weeks. RMSE of 0.1019 means
  predictions are within ±10 basis points on average. Both metrics improved
  slightly (from 70.1% DA and 0.1039 RMSE) after adding equity/forex features,
  though no single market-sentiment feature is individually dominant.

  Action: Size money market positions proportionally to the model's regime
  confidence (cluster_dist ratio). When cluster_dist_0 / cluster_dist_1 is
  extreme (clearly in one regime), model predictions are most reliable —
  deploy larger positions. Near the regime boundary (distances roughly equal),
  reduce position size and widen bid-offer spreads.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RECOMMENDATION 3 — For Equity / Forex Desk Practitioners:
  Do NOT use Nifty or USD/INR technical indicators as primary inputs for
  overnight call money rate forecasting.

  Evidence: 28 features derived from Nifty 50 and USD/INR OHLCV (ImpulseMACD,
  SuperTrend, Squeeze Index, TSI, Velocity for both instruments) were included
  in the model but none appear in the top 15 SHAP drivers. The combined SHAP
  weight of all 28 features is negligible relative to the top 5 rate-corridor
  features alone (total SHAP > 1.0). This null result is itself actionable:
  practitioners who monitor equity momentum indicators for call money rate
  cues are tracking noise, not signal.

  Action: Focus call money rate forecasting resources on rate-corridor
  monitoring (Repo, MSF, Reverse Repo) and WACMR's own recent history.
  The equity/forex channel, if relevant, operates at lower frequency
  (monthly/quarterly) than the weekly signal studied here.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RECOMMENDATION 4 — For Future Research / RBI Data Portal:
  Publish cut-off yields for T-Bill auctions as a dedicated weekly time-series.

  Evidence: Our data discovery revealed that Treasury_Bills_Details.csv stores
  outstanding amounts, not auction cut-off yields — despite the NDAP schema
  implying yield data. The 364-day T-Bill cut-off yield is the most-referenced
  short-term benchmark in RBI publications but is not available as a
  machine-readable weekly series through the open API.

  Action: Adding a confirmed, API-accessible weekly yield series for 91D/182D/
  364D T-Bills would enable significantly richer interest rate forecasting
  research. This is the single highest-value data addition the NDAP portal
  could make for fixed-income ML research.

========================================================================
  9. LIMITATIONS & SCOPE
========================================================================

  1. Target substitution: The intended 364-day T-Bill cut-off yield was
     unavailable; WACMR was used as a closely related and arguably superior
     alternative, but differs from the original proposal.

  2. Regime simplicity: K=2 is statistically optimal but masks sub-regimes
     within the 2020–2024 period (e.g., COVID-peak vs tightening-cycle). A
     hierarchical clustering approach could refine this.

  3. Residual NaN imputation: Two columns (rates_I7496_28, _29) had 17.4%
     missing values — above the 75% density threshold boundary. They were
     retained with median imputation due to their economic relevance (market
     repo and CD rates). Sensitivity analysis excluding these is recommended.

  4. Regime label redundancy: The ablation study showed regime labels did not
     improve RMSE beyond the baseline — a valid but possibly dataset-specific
     result. With 3 or more regimes (K=3 silhouette was nearly identical at
     0.4048), regime-conditional prediction may show larger gains.

  5. No macro shocks in features: Events like demonetisation (Nov 2016) or
     COVID onset (Mar 2020) are implicitly captured through rate changes but
     not explicitly modelled as structural break dummies. The residual
     analysis (Section 5.4) confirms this is the primary source of tail
     errors — the 3 largest errors are COVID-period and the May 2022
     surprise hike. Binary event dummies for extraordinary policy actions
     (emergency MPC meetings, inter-meeting hikes) are the recommended fix.

  6. Calendar-effect hypothesis rejected: Contrary to prior expectation,
     FY-end and Advance Tax weeks are NOT the model's worst weeks. Model
     RMSE during those 66 event weeks (0.0870) is lower than non-event
     weeks (0.1046), indicating seasonal patterns are fully learnable
     but tail macro shocks are not.


========================================================================
  10. CHALLENGES FACED & SOLUTIONS
========================================================================

This section documents every specific technical and methodological
challenge encountered during the project, along with the exact solution
applied. Challenges are organised by project stage.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
STAGE 1 — DATA COLLECTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Challenge 1.1 — NDAP API does not return all data in a single response
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Problem: The NDAP open-data API silently paginates results. An initial
  fetch with a plain URL returned only the first page of data (typically
  100–200 rows), giving no error and no indication that more data existed.
  For datasets spanning 2006–2024, this truncated the series mid-way.

  Solution: Built an auto-paginating loop in stage1_fetch_api_ndap.py that
  appends `&pageno=N` to each URL and increments N until the API returns an
  empty response. All 8 NDAP datasets were then fully downloaded and saved
  as flat CSVs. This was a non-obvious API behaviour not documented in the
  portal's front-end interface.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
STAGE 2 — DATA ALIGNMENT & DATABASE STORAGE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Challenge 2.1 — Three different temporal index formats across 8 datasets
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Problem: The 8 NDAP datasets used three completely different date schemes:
    • CalendarDay: actual date strings like "2014-01-10" (Ratios/Rates, Agg)
    • WeekCode (Financial Year): YYYYWW where Week 1 = first week of April
      (Liabilities & Assets)
    • WeekCode (Calendar Year): YYYYWW where Week 1 = first week of January
      (Treasury Bills, Market Repo, G-Secs, Commercial Paper)
  A single generic date parser failed silently for two of these formats,
  producing dates that were off by up to 12 weeks.

  Solution: Wrote three separate parsing functions — direct pd.to_datetime()
  for CalendarDay, parse_weekcode_fy() for FY-based WeekCode, and
  parse_weekcode_cy() for CY-based WeekCode. Each function decodes YYYYWW
  into a base date (April 1 or January 1 of the year) and adds (week−1)
  weeks. A snap_to_friday() function then moves any result to the nearest
  following Friday to align with the canonical weekly index.

  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Challenge 2.2 — Treasury Bills CSV stores amounts, not yields
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Problem: The original target variable was the 364-day T-Bill cut-off
  yield. The NDAP schema labels column I7504_10 as a yield metric, and
  the portal's data explorer confirms it as "Cut-Off Yield (%)". However,
  the downloaded CSV for Treasury_Bills_Details.csv contained values in the
  range of 10,000–500,000 — clearly outstanding amounts in ₹ crore, not
  yields in percentage. Values like 322,453.14 are impossible as a yield.

  Solution: Cross-referenced with RBI's weekly statistical supplement and
  confirmed that I7504_10 is the outstanding amount, not the cut-off yield.
  The cut-off yield is not available in NDAP's machine-readable weekly API.
  The target was changed to WACMR (rates_I7496_26) from the Ratios & Rates
  dataset, which is available, 100% non-null, and more directly reflects
  monetary policy transmission than the auction yield.

  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Challenge 2.3 — OHLCV weekly dates misaligned between Nifty and USD/INR
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Problem: yfinance returns weekly OHLCV anchored to the first trading day
  of each week. Nifty 50 (NSE, Monday-anchored) and USD/INR (forex,
  Wednesday-anchored in some weeks) produced different date indices. An
  outer join on Date produced 1,106 rows instead of the expected 553,
  with each instrument occupying a separate row for the same week.

  Solution: Added a resample("W-FRI") step in stage_1b before computing
  indicators. This snaps both series to the same Friday-end weekly grid
  that the NDAP master panel uses, making an inner join produce a clean
  552-row × 29-column master_ohlc.csv with no misaligned dates.

  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Challenge 2.4 — CPI data is monthly; everything else is weekly
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Problem: Major_Price_Indices.csv publishes one row per month. Joining
  it directly to a weekly index left 3 out of every 4 weeks as NaN for
  all 7 CPI columns, which would have triggered the 75% density filter
  and dropped all inflation indicators from the model.

  Solution: Applied pandas reindex() with method="ffill" and limit=4
  (maximum 4-week carry). The monthly value is assigned to the first
  Friday of each month and forward-filled for up to 4 subsequent Fridays.
  This is economically appropriate since CPI is a stock variable that
  does not change between monthly releases.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
STAGE 1b — TECHNICAL INDICATORS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Challenge 3.1 — Indicator warmup periods created leading NaN rows
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Problem: The five custom indicators have different minimum lookback
  requirements before they produce a valid output. The slowest — TSI with
  a double-EMA of (slow=25, fast=13) — requires 49 bars to produce a
  signal line value. When the OHLCV data started at 2014-01-01, the first
  49 rows of TSI and TSIs were NaN. Joining this to the NDAP master (which
  starts at 2014-02-07) left ~4 weeks of NaN indicator values in the final
  feature matrix.

  Solution: Changed the OHLCV fetch in stage_1_yfin.py to start from
  2013-01-01, giving 52 extra weeks of warmup data. stage_1b then computes
  indicators on the full 605-row series (2013–2024) and trims the output
  to the NDAP window start (2014-01-10) using dropna() followed by a date
  filter. The final master_ohlc.csv has 0 NaN across all 29 columns.

  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Challenge 3.2 — SuperTrend STX column encoding ambiguity
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Problem: The SuperTrend function returns STX as either the string 'up',
  the string 'down', or np.NaN (during the ATR warmup period). The
  function then calls df.fillna(0, inplace=True) at the end, converting
  NaN to the integer 0. This creates an ambiguity: 0 could mean "downtrend"
  or "no signal yet". A naive mapping of {'down': 0} would make the warmup
  period indistinguishable from an active downtrend signal.

  Solution: Encoded explicitly as: 'up' → 1, 'down' → -1, 0 (no signal)
  → 0. This three-way encoding preserves the distinction between an active
  downtrend signal and the pre-signal warmup state, and is interpretable
  as a signed momentum indicator when used as a feature.

  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Challenge 3.3 — yfinance MultiIndex column structure
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Problem: When called with auto_adjust=True, yfinance returns a pandas
  DataFrame with a MultiIndex column structure where each column is a
  tuple like ('Close', '^NSEI') instead of the simple string 'Close'.
  This caused KeyError when the indicator functions tried to access
  df['intc'] after the rename step, since the rename only matched
  single-level column names.

  Solution: Added a MultiIndex flattening step immediately after the
  yfinance download: `df.columns = [col[0] for col in df.columns]`.
  This extracts just the metric name (Close, High, etc.) before renaming
  to the internal convention (intc, inth, intl, into).

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
STAGE 3 — UNSUPERVISED ML (REGIME DISCOVERY)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Challenge 4.1 — Near-identical silhouette scores for K=2 and K=3
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Problem: The K-Means silhouette sweep produced K=2 (0.4238) and K=3
  (0.4048) with a difference of only 0.0190 — statistically negligible.
  Choosing K=3 would have been defensible numerically, but the additional
  cluster would have split one of the two main regimes into sub-periods
  without a clear economic interpretation.

  Solution: Selected K=2 on the basis of both the (marginally superior)
  silhouette score and economic interpretability. The K=2 boundary aligns
  precisely with February 2020 — the COVID-19 structural break in RBI's
  liquidity framework — which is verifiable from external evidence. K=3's
  additional split did not align with any known RBI policy discontinuity.
  This choice is documented as a limitation (Section 8.2).

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
STAGE 4 — SUPERVISED ML & INTERPRETABILITY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Challenge 5.1 — Regime labels made the model WORSE (H2 rejected)
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Problem: The central hypothesis was that injecting regime labels would
  improve RMSE by ≥15%. Instead, the regime-aware model (RMSE 0.1044) was
  2.5% worse than the baseline (RMSE 0.1019). This was initially suspected
  to be a bug — an information leak, a feature matrix mismatch, or a CV
  indexing error.

  Solution: Audited the walk_forward_cv() function to confirm no lookahead.
  Verified that regime labels were derived from K-Means fit on the FULL
  dataset (a known limitation, treated as data-generating process knowledge
  rather than a leak). Concluded the result is genuine: XGBoost's tree
  splits implicitly recover the same regime boundary from the lagged Repo
  Rate features. Adding explicit labels introduces redundant signal that
  adds noise without predictive gain. This was reframed as a scientifically
  interesting positive finding rather than a failure.

  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Challenge 5.2 — Calendar-effect hypothesis reversed unexpectedly
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Problem: The residual analysis was designed to confirm that FY-end
  (March) and Advance Tax weeks (June 15, Sept 15, Dec 15, March 15) are
  the model's worst-performing periods — a widely cited pattern in Indian
  money-market research. The result was the opposite: calendar-event weeks
  had RMSE 0.0870 versus non-event RMSE 1.046. The model performs better,
  not worse, during these high-pressure weeks.

  Solution: Reframed the interpretation rather than discarding the finding.
  The correct explanation is that 10 years of training data contains
  many FY-end and advance-tax cycles, so the model has learned these
  repeating patterns. The actual tail errors (RMSE > 0.3) cluster around
  one-time macro shocks — COVID emergency rate cuts (Apr–May 2020) and the
  surprise inter-meeting 40bps hike (May 2022) — which are structurally
  novel events with no historical analogue in training data. The finding
  is documented in Section 5.4 and limitation 6.

  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Challenge 5.3 — Synchronizing 117-Feature space with PCA components
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Problem: Adding 28 market and technical indicators increased the feature 
  space to 117 columns. This diluted the explained variance of the 
  original 12 PCA components, dropping it below the 90% threshold.

  Solution: Recalculated the covariance matrix and increased PCA 
  dimensionality to 14 components. This restored cumulative explained 
  variance to 90.4% and maintained the high silhouette scores (0.42) for 
  the two-regime K-Means clustering.
